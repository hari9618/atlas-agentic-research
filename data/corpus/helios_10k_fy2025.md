PART I — BUSINESS

Helios Robotics Inc. ("Helios", "we", "the Company") designs and manufactures
autonomous warehouse robots and the fleet-orchestration software that coordinates
them. The Company operates in two reportable segments: Robotics Hardware and
Fleet Software. Founded in 2014 and headquartered in Austin, Texas, Helios serves
logistics, e-commerce, and third-party fulfillment customers across North America
and Europe.

FINANCIAL HIGHLIGHTS

For the fiscal year ended December 31, 2025, total revenue was $4.2 billion, an
increase of 28% compared to $3.28 billion in fiscal 2024. Robotics Hardware revenue
was $2.9 billion and Fleet Software revenue was $1.3 billion. Software revenue grew
46% year over year and now represents 31% of total revenue, reflecting the Company's
strategic shift toward higher-margin recurring revenue. Gross margin expanded to
41%, up from 36% in the prior year. The Company reported net income of $312 million
and ended the year with $1.8 billion in cash and cash equivalents.

Annual recurring revenue (ARR) for Fleet Software reached $1.5 billion, with a net
revenue retention rate of 122%. The Company served 540 enterprise customers at year
end, up from 410 a year earlier.

RISK FACTORS

Supplier concentration. A substantial portion of our robots depend on high-precision
actuators sourced from a single supplier located in Taiwan. Any disruption to this
supplier — whether from geopolitical tension, natural disaster, or capacity
constraints — could materially delay product shipments and harm our results. We are
qualifying a second source but do not expect meaningful dual-sourcing before late 2026.

Customer concentration. Our three largest customers accounted for 39% of total
revenue in fiscal 2025. The loss of any one of them would significantly reduce revenue.

Competition. The autonomous warehouse robotics market is intensely competitive. Our
principal competitor is Aster Dynamics Corp., which has greater financial resources
and a larger installed base in Europe. We also compete with several well-funded
startups and with the in-house automation programs of large retailers.

Regulatory. Evolving safety regulations for autonomous machinery in shared human
workspaces could increase compliance costs and slow deployments.
